# Left 4 Dead 2 domain reference

This document is the shared game-domain reference for L4DStats. Its emphasis is
Versus, competitive play, SourceTV demos, and what can be inferred safely from
recorded server state. It is not a strategy guide and it does not treat community
config behavior as vanilla behavior.

Last researched: 17 July 2026.

## The game in one model

Left 4 Dead 2 is a team-based Source-engine FPS built around four Survivors moving
through a directed map while the AI Director and an opposing infected force create
pressure. Position along map flow matters more than conventional territory. The
fundamental unit is not a deathmatch life; it is a four-person attempt to convert
health, time, inventory, spacing, and coordinated control into forward distance.

The main families of modes are:

| Family           | Core idea                                           | Relevance to L4DStats                             |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Campaign / Co-op | Humans play Survivors against AI infected           | Useful parser baseline; not symmetric competition |
| Versus           | Teams alternate Survivor and Special Infected sides | Primary analytical target                         |
| Realism          | Reduced assists and harsher information rules       | Different interpretation of awareness evidence    |
| Scavenge         | Alternating fuel-can offense and infected defense   | Different objectives, rounds, and scoring         |
| Survival         | Hold a fixed area for time                          | Flow distance is not the objective                |
| Mutations        | Script/config variants of the base rules            | Must be identified before comparing statistics    |

## Versus

### Match and round structure

A conventional Versus match has two teams, normally four players each. On every
chapter/map:

1. Team A plays Survivors while Team B controls Special Infected.
2. The Survivor attempt ends when the team reaches the saferoom/end condition or
   no active Survivor can continue.
3. The sides swap on the same chapter.
4. Both teams therefore attack and defend the same route, reducing - but not
   eliminating - Director, item, Tank, Witch, and execution variance.
5. Chapter scores accumulate into the match score.

Finales, gauntlets, holdouts, elevators, crescendos, and map transitions can change
the exact end condition. A parser should derive round boundaries from events and
state rather than assume that every map is a simple saferoom-to-saferoom walk.

### What wins

Vanilla Versus rewards Survivor progress and successful completion, with the
engine/config determining exact distance, survival, tie-break, and chapter-score
details. Competitive configs commonly replace or reshape that calculation with a
health bonus and fixed map-distance model. Never compare score components until
the match configuration is known.

Useful conceptual decomposition:

```text
chapter result
  = map progress
  + completion/survival outcome
  + config-specific health or damage bonus
  + config-specific penalties, tie breaks, or adjustments
```

For analysis, retain both raw engine score fields and independently derived facts:
furthest flow, each Survivor's terminal flow, alive/incapacitated state, permanent
and temporary health, and the event sequence. Do not back-solve one scoring system
from another.

### Survivor-side objective

Survivors must preserve a moving four-player formation while progressing. Their
resources include:

- permanent health and temporary health;
- incapacitation and ledge-hang capacity;
- primary and secondary weapons, ammunition, melee weapons, and upgrades;
- first-aid kits, defibrillators, pills, adrenaline, throwables, and carried
  objective items;
- shove/melee cooldown, reload state, movement speed, and line of sight;
- knowledge from sound, outlines, voice communication, prior spawns, and map
  experience.

The important tactical states are not merely alive/dead. They include separated,
pinned, stumbled, pounced, ridden, charged, smoked, boomed, spat on, incapacitated,
hanging, black-and-white, revived, rescued, and under Tank pressure.

### Infected-side objective

The infected team tries to convert renewable lives into irreversible Survivor
resource loss and a shorter run. Ordinary Special Infected attacks are strongest
as a synchronized **hit** rather than four unrelated spawns. Typical structure:

1. collect a viable set of classes and spawn positions;
2. wait for a choke, distraction, common horde, event, Witch, or Tank;
3. establish line blockers/displacement;
4. land simultaneous or closely sequenced controls;
5. deal damage, force item use, separate players, or cause an instant death;
6. die quickly enough to re-enter the spawn cycle when continued survival has no
   tactical value.

This makes infected deaths a poor standalone performance measure. Spawn-cycle
time, damage per life, controls landed, duration of pins, separation created,
skeets/dead-stops against them, and damage converted by teammates are more useful.

### Ghost and spawn state

Human-controlled Special Infected usually enter a non-corporeal ghost state before
spawning. A valid spawn is constrained by server rules and map geometry, including
distance, Survivor visibility, nav areas, checkpoint/safe-area exclusions, spawn
volumes, and class limits. Competitive plugins may modify spawn timers, class
selection, limits, or frustration behavior.

Analytics must distinguish:

- waiting/dead;
- ghost roaming;
- spawn committed;
- alive and actionable;
- pinned or stumbled;
- ability cooldown/recovery;
- despawn, suicide, kick, bot takeover, or player replacement.

Ghost position is strategic intent, not a physical infected location. Including it
in movement, teleport, or aim-opponent calculations will create nonsense.

### Special Infected

| Class   | Primary role                             | Signature state/evidence                                         | Common analytical traps                                                     |
| ------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Smoker  | Long-range pull and separation           | tongue launch, connect, pull, choke, tongue break                | Treating obstructed pull path as wall knowledge; ignoring tongue sound      |
| Boomer  | Blind/debuff, horde trigger, area denial | vomit attempt, bile coverage, popped-on target, horde response   | Counting zero direct damage as zero impact; missing proxy/pop booms         |
| Hunter  | Mobile pounce and high pin damage        | leap, airborne path, pounce, high-pounce distance                | Confusing practice movement with attack; config-dependent damage caps       |
| Spitter | Area denial and damage amplification     | spit projectile, pool start/end, damage ticks                    | Attributing all spit damage to aim; ignoring pins that hold targets in acid |
| Jockey  | Short-range control and steering         | ride start/end, victim displacement                              | Ignoring victim movement caused by the Jockey or rescue geometry            |
| Charger | Line-breaking displacement and carry     | charge, impact, carry, pummel, multi-hit                         | Treating forced movement as player input; instant-kill geometry             |
| Tank    | Boss pressure, hittables, rocks, punches | control assignment, rock throws, punches, fire, frustration/pass | Config-dependent health/control; hittable ownership and chip damage         |
| Witch   | Neutral boss hazard                      | startle, target, attack, crown, burn                             | Director/map spawn rather than infected-player class; shotgun crown context |

Vanilla health, cooldowns, damage, velocity, class limits, and spawn timers are
cvar-controlled and are frequently changed by competitive configs. Store the
effective values or config fingerprint; do not bake remembered defaults into an
evidence model.

### Common infected and the Director

Common infected create chip damage, movement blocking, target switching, shove
pressure, and cover for Special Infected. The AI Director regulates population,
mob timing, item placement, bosses, and other pacing elements. The Left 4 Dead
engine branch uses NextBot navigation meshes and an AI Director rather than a
fixed encounter script for every detail.

Consequences for analysis:

- identical maps do not imply identical encounters;
- an aim snap to a common infected can be legitimate target switching;
- horde noise and occlusion alter what a player can reasonably know;
- Boomer bile and scripted panic events change population pressure;
- nav flow is more stable than Euclidean distance for progress;
- Director RNG must not be mislabeled as player skill or misconduct.

### Tanks and Witches

Boss presence strongly partitions a round. Tank play has phases: spawn/commit,
approach, chip/rock pressure, hittable setup, punch commit, fire state, control
pass or frustration, and death. Survivor decisions depend on retreat space,
burn source, hittables, common support, remaining Special Infected, and whether a
one-way drop has been crossed.

A Witch encounter depends on placement, path width, illumination, agitation,
weapon availability, and whether the team chooses bypass, burn, stumble, or crown.
Boss prohibition nav/map regions and competitive boss-flow rules can change where
bosses are allowed. Record actual spawn/control facts rather than infer them solely
from map reputation.

## Competitive Versus

Public vanilla Versus and organized competitive Versus are different analytical
populations. Confogl-derived configurations and ZoneMod commonly standardize or
rebalance item density, weapon tiers, boss behavior, scoring, infected spawns,
ready-up, pauses, match halves, and bug fixes. Plugins can also emit additional
events or alter entity behavior.

The actively maintained
[L4D2 Competitive Rework repository](https://github.com/SirPlease/L4D2-Competitive-Rework)
contains multiple configs and continuous fixes; “competitive” is not one immutable
ruleset. CEDAPug may change the selected config over time. For every demo set,
capture where available:

- server hostname and build/protocol;
- game mode and mutation;
- `mp_gamemode` and relevant cvars;
- SourceMod/plugin inventory and versions;
- Confogl/ZoneMod/config name and checksum;
- ready-up/live transition;
- team rosters, substitutions, and side order;
- pause/unpause, restart, restore, and technical-disruption events;
- map/stripper version and custom-campaign identity.

### Competitive vocabulary

| Term               | Meaning                                                                             |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| hit                | Coordinated Special Infected attack cycle                                           |
| sack               | Deliberately spend an infected life to improve class/cycle/timing                   |
| clear              | Free a controlled Survivor                                                          |
| skeet              | Kill an airborne Hunter before the pounce lands                                     |
| dead-stop          | Shove a Hunter out of its pounce before pin                                         |
| level              | Shoot a Charger during its charge before meaningful impact                          |
| crown              | Kill a Witch at/near startle, usually with a close shotgun burst                    |
| chip               | Safe ranged damage, especially against a Tank                                       |
| hittable           | Physics prop a Tank can punch into Survivors                                        |
| LOS                | Line of sight; also shorthand for breaking Tank rock/attack vision                  |
| flow               | Directed progress through the nav mesh                                              |
| choke              | Geometry that compresses movement or limits clears/spawns                           |
| one-way            | Drop or transition that prevents easy retreat                                       |
| wipe               | Entire Survivor team loses the ability to continue                                  |
| perm / temp health | Permanent hit points versus decaying buffer health                                  |
| bleed-out          | Temporary-health decay / incap health pressure                                      |
| black-and-white    | Survivor will die on the next incapacitation under normal rules                     |
| lerp               | Client interpolation delay; relevant to perceived timing, not directly demoed input |

Vocabulary is context, not a detector label. For example, a “skeet” requires an
airborne Hunter, a shot/impact sequence, and a death before control - not merely a
large view-angle change near a Hunter.

## Survivors, inventory, and damage

The playable L4D2 Survivor set is Coach, Ellis, Nick, and Rochelle. Ported L4D1
campaigns use Bill, Francis, Louis, and Zoey. Character model is not stable player
identity: teams swap sides, bots take over, and clients can reconnect.

Weapon families include pistols and magnum; SMGs; pump/chrome and automatic/combat
shotguns; rifles; hunting and military sniper rifles; grenade launcher; M60; and
multiple melee weapons. Throwables are pipe bomb, Molotov, and bile jar. Medical
items include first aid, defibrillator, pills, and adrenaline. Laser sights,
incendiary ammo, and explosive ammo may appear depending on map/config.

Damage attribution must handle:

- friendly fire and difficulty/config scaling;
- pellets versus a single trigger pull;
- projectile travel and splash;
- fire ownership and persistent world damage;
- spit ticks and combined pin damage;
- fall, crush, drown, ledge, and kill-volume damage;
- Tank rocks and physics hittables;
- common/Special Infected contribution to the same incap;
- temporary health and healing after damage;
- incapacitation versus final death.

## Maps, campaigns, and flow

A competitive game spans a campaign sequence of chapter maps. SourceTV normally
produces one demo per loaded map, not one file for the whole game. The demo
format has no universal match UUID. L4DStats therefore reconstructs a game from
the protected embedded server identity, the stable eight-player roster, adjacent
Source server-generation counters, and campaign/chapter sequence. Filenames are
not evidence. See
[ADR 0008](docs/decisions/0008-game-session-reconstruction.md) for the exact
confidence and rematch boundaries.

Current official content includes the original L4D2 campaigns plus ported L4D1
campaigns and later additions. Common map prefixes are:

| Prefix | Campaign       |
| ------ | -------------- |
| `c1`   | Dead Center    |
| `c2`   | Dark Carnival  |
| `c3`   | Swamp Fever    |
| `c4`   | Hard Rain      |
| `c5`   | The Parish     |
| `c6`   | The Passing    |
| `c7`   | The Sacrifice  |
| `c8`   | No Mercy       |
| `c9`   | Crash Course   |
| `c10`  | Death Toll     |
| `c11`  | Dead Air       |
| `c12`  | Blood Harvest  |
| `c13`  | Cold Stream    |
| `c14`  | The Last Stand |

Custom campaigns can use arbitrary names and modified official maps can preserve
misleading prefixes. Identify maps by demo header, mission metadata, BSP/hash where
available, and config/stripper lineage - not name alone.

Nav meshes carry traversal and semantic attributes such as flow, checkpoints,
finales, spawn restrictions, and infected-only ladders. Flow distance is the best
common coordinate for Survivor progress, but it is not sufficient for vertical
overlaps, alternate routes, teleports, elevators, or maps with broken/custom nav.

## Source engine and demos

L4D2 uses Valve's Left 4 Dead Source branch. Relevant properties include VPK
content, BSP v21, VScript/Squirrel, NextBot nav meshes, the AI Director, string
tables, send tables, entity baselines, and delta-compressed packet entities. Valve
notes that demo compatibility is tied to network/patch versions; old demos may not
play in a current client even when their outer container remains parseable.

A Source demo contains recorded server/network messages, not raw mouse movement,
the complete client render, voice semantics, or the player's subjective screen.
A SourceTV competitive demo is an observer-oriented server record. Depending on
capture and protocol, useful material includes:

- header: map, game directory, server/client labels, playback time/ticks/frames;
- sign-on and server info;
- send tables and server classes;
- instance baselines and packet-entity deltas;
- string tables, especially `userinfo` changes;
- game-event schema and event instances;
- console commands, data-table messages, sounds, temp entities, and user messages;
- observer/server ticks and sequence framing.

It does **not** directly prove:

- physical mouse movement or sensitivity;
- exact pixels visible on a player's monitor;
- client FPS, frame pacing, display latency, or input latency;
- all client-side prediction/interpolation artifacts;
- voice-chat content or external team communication;
- what a player consciously noticed;
- authoritative occlusion without matching map collision/dynamic state;
- audibility without sound origin, mix, occlusion, and client context;
- stable real-world identity without careful userinfo epoch handling.

### Tick and time

Use engine/demo tick as the primary coordinate. Derived seconds are useful for
display, rates, and windows, but pauses, restarts, skips, time scaling, and corrupt
headers can break a naive constant wall-clock mapping. Preserve:

- demo command tick;
- decoded server tick where available;
- header playback ticks/time;
- tick interval derivation and confidence;
- discontinuities and pause/restart boundaries.

### Players and identity

Player slot, entity index, user ID, character, and Steam identity are different:

- entity indices can be reused;
- a `userid` is connection-scoped;
- player slots and teams change;
- bot takeover changes controller without necessarily replacing the character;
- reconnects create a new connection epoch;
- character does not identify a human;
- raw platform identity is sensitive and should not enter reports.

L4DStats keys observations by a demo-local player epoch and uses a keyed,
privacy-preserving token only when cross-demo association is justified. Preserve
userinfo change provenance and expose rejected/ambiguous mappings.

## Events and state useful to L4DStats

Event names vary by build/config and an event is not always a complete state
transition. High-value families include:

| Family             | Examples / intent                                              |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| lifecycle          | round start/end, map transition, finale start, mission lost    |
| identity/team      | connect, disconnect, team change, bot replace/takeover         |
| combat             | weapon fire, bullet impact, hurt, death, incap, revive, heal   |
| control            | pounce, tongue, charge, pummel, ride, release/clear            |
| Boomer             | vomit, bile coverage, Boomer death/pop                         |
| Spitter/fire       | spit, inferno start/burn/extinguish, periodic damage           |
| bosses             | Tank spawn/control/rock/punch/death, Witch spawn/startle/death |
| inventory          | pickup, drop, use, ammo/upgrade, throwable use                 |
| movement/objective | ledge grab, saferoom/checkpoint, scavenge can events           |
| match ops          | ready/live, pause, restart, restore - often plugin-defined     |

State projection should reconcile events with entity properties. Events provide
semantic edges; packet entities provide sampled state. Either can be absent,
duplicated, delayed, or config-specific.

### Capture perspective and command intent

SourceTV and player-recorded demos share the protocol-2100 container but expose
different viewpoints. SourceTV is a server-observed multi-player reconstruction.
A player-POV demo additionally records `dem_usercmd` for one recorder: command
angles, movement intent, buttons, weapon selection and mouse deltas. Those
values are client submissions, not proof that the server accepted an action or
that it produced a shot, hit, displacement or damage outcome.

Analysis therefore preserves three layers separately: recorder command intent,
server-observed state, and event/outcome evidence. Both demo tick and client
command tick are retained. Perspective is a prerequisite for every input-based
statistic, and POV-only evidence is never generalized to teammates, opponents,
or SourceTV recordings. Exact physical mouse motion, rendered crosshair,
recoil compensation and bullet spread remain unavailable.

## Statistical model for a Versus deep dive

### Match/chapter

- round duration and active-play duration;
- team side order and chapter result;
- flow progress curve and terminal flow;
- Tank/Witch spawn flow and encounter duration;
- common/SI damage, friendly fire, incaps, deaths, and item consumption;
- pause/restart/discontinuity markers;
- config and data-availability fingerprint.

### Survivor

- active time, alive/incap/hanging/dead time;
- permanent/temp health trajectory;
- flow speed, stop time, separation, formation distance, and backtracking;
- shots, accuracy only where defensibly reconstructable, friendly fire, clears;
- SI controls suffered, clear latency, avoidable damage context;
- item pickup/use/pass and weapon timeline;
- Tank/Witch engagement context;
- view travel and target transitions with reconstruction quality.

### Special Infected

- ghost/wait/alive cycle duration;
- class sequence and spawn location relative to flow;
- attack participation and synchronization window;
- controls attempted/landed, pin duration, displacement, direct and converted damage;
- life duration, death cause, skeet/dead-stop/level context;
- Boomer coverage and horde conversion;
- Tank control, rocks, punches, hittables, fire, pass/frustration.

### Team coordination

- Survivor pairwise spacing and isolation duration;
- clear coverage and clear latency;
- focus-fire timing;
- infected hit concurrency, class composition, and damage conversion;
- resource exchange before/after major events;
- repeated behavior across independent rounds/demos.

Every rate needs a denominator: per minute, per attack, per life, per eligible
window, per shot, or per unit flow. Raw counts reward longer rounds and are rarely
comparable by themselves.

An attack-command denominator is not a shot denominator. Held attack can span
several automatic shots or no legal shot, while shotguns, melee, chainsaw,
throwables, mounted weapons and infected abilities have distinct semantics.
Accuracy remains unavailable until both fired-shot and hit/miss outcomes are
validated for the relevant weapon and capture perspective.

## Cheating-analysis lens

L4DStats may surface interesting moments, but Versus creates extreme legitimate
behavior: rehearsed pre-aim at spawn routes, flicks between simultaneous pins,
wall-adjacent tracking from audio, instant target selection from teammate calls,
and precise choke timing learned over thousands of matches.

Potential signal families include:

- view-angle dynamics inconsistent with surrounding samples;
- repeated target acquisition before observable cues;
- implausibly stable tracking across target acceleration/occlusion;
- reaction-time distributions conditional on genuine first information;
- recoil/spread outcomes inconsistent across sufficiently large comparable samples;
- movement or action timing impossible under authoritative server state;
- cross-demo persistence after controlling for map/choke/config/player role.

Required counterevidence/context includes:

- sound and vocalization cues;
- glow/outlines and spectator-versus-player information differences;
- teammate voice calls;
- common pre-aim locations and spawn timing;
- demo sampling/interpolation and angle quantization;
- recoil, spread, shove, stumble, pin, charge, or Jockey-forced movement;
- lag, loss, prediction, and server corrections;
- class/weapon sensitivity and scoped state;
- target selection among overlapping infected;
- map geometry, penetrable surfaces, and dynamic props;
- practice, skill, and repeated-route knowledge.

No single clip, snap, score, or detector family supports a cheating probability.
The safe output is a reproducible review window with tick range, evidence quality,
limitations, strongest benign explanation, and independent corroboration status.

## Engineering invariants

1. Identify game mode/config before comparing demos.
2. Separate vanilla facts, config-derived facts, and observed demo facts.
3. Treat map, nav, plugin, and protocol versions as data dependencies.
4. Use tick as primary time and flow as primary progress when valid.
5. Separate player identity, connection epoch, entity, character, and controller.
6. Separate ghost intent from spawned infected state.
7. Attribute forced movement and damage sources explicitly.
8. Retain missing/unavailable fields; never turn them into zero.
9. Preserve source hashes and derivation lineage for every statistic.
10. Present cheating-related output as secondary review signals, never verdicts.

## Questions the parser should answer before detectors run

- Is this actually L4D2, and which network/demo protocol is present?
- Is it SourceTV, POV, or another capture form?
- What map, mode, mutation, server, and config can be established?
- Where are round/live/pause/restart boundaries?
- Are send tables, baselines, string tables, userinfo, events, and packet entities
  complete enough for the requested statistic?
- Which entity classes and properties were observed, derived, or unavailable?
- Can player epochs be mapped without slot-reuse errors?
- Is tick-to-time continuous and credible?
- Is nav flow/map geometry available and version-matched?
- Did plugins alter rules or emit non-vanilla events?

If any prerequisite is missing, the dependent statistic or detector must skip with
a structured reason.
